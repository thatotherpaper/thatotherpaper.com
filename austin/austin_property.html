I own a lot of Austin property — in the future

24 February 2007 | Jimmy Waugh
photo / Ling Li Yeoh 

I’d been dating this girl for three years, but her father wouldn’t let her marry me because I didn’t own a house. Don’t ask me why – it’s a Moldavian thing. Listen, I live in Austin. I was a math major. I work at Taco Shack. The only house I could afford around here is under a snow globe at The Cadeau. Nevermind, I probably can’t afford that either. The point is, I had to get creative. So I did: I bought Austin real estate in the fourth dimension.

I have purchased some enormous and fabulous properties – in the future. I have the Prairie-style manse on a half-acre over in Bee Caves and the condo downtown (below Fourth, of course). I also started that limited partnership with a few rentals in Kyle. New construction, so I don’t have to worry about maintenance. There’s also a time-share in Oaxaca – but who counts time shares? All told, I have future personal assets in excess of three million dollars.

How is this possible? I’m glad you asked. All sorts of people are making serious coin these days playing the future market. Hedge funds, investment banks, pension funds – everybody is in the game, putting their money down on next year’s stock market bust, a hurricane that’s going to happen in 2009, and some fantastic oil reserves somebody is going to discover, somewhere, in 2013. This isn’t pie in the sky, friends. This is real value. Real money.

I think we can agree that I’m a man of above-average intelligence, young, and in good health. I have fantastic “room for growth” as we like to say in the future market. It’s also beyond dispute that there are wonderful real estate properties already existing – and yet to be built – in Austin that I would very much like to own. Future sellers of those properties will be eager to know a married man of future means such as myself. Put these facts together and it follows, as logically as supply follows demand, that I do possess future personal assets in excess of three million dollars.

This is sleight-of-hand, you say – an accounting trick. Not so. All of this is based on the well-known fact that money has time-value. Money changes in value over time. Normally, a given amount of money will decrease in value in the future, unless one factors in a rate of interest. But as I already said, I am interested in being married. What greater rate of interest can be imagined?

photo / jmpk 

I’m joking, of course. I don’t know from interest. The mathematics are too complex to explain here, but suffice to say with the reversal of a few arithmetical terms in the standard formula, my real estate holdings in the fourth dimension are rock solid assets, as sure as the Bevo Burrito is tasty.

So, bottom line? I am finally engaged. By this time next year I might be a shift manager at Schlotzky’s if a buddy of my roommate comes through. Unfortunately, my enormous, improbable wealth (improbable as a matter of physics, not finance) has caused a few minor headaches. Namely, tax headaches. The tax bill is ridiculous on future personal assets in excess of three million dollars.

Luckily, though, thanks to my insights into fourth-dimensional real estate, I’ve been able to offset it with the depreciation from my great-great-great-great-great-great grandfather’s castle in Scotland.
