The Mist

23 November 2007 | Bill Wine
courtesy The Mist official site  

Is The Mist not to be missed?

Afraid not.

Horrormeister Stephen King has written novels, novellas, and short stories that have been the source for movies great, so-so, and dreadful.

And The Mist director Frank Darabont has been responsible, as screenwriter and director, for two of the best: The Green Mile and The Shawshank Redemption, each screenplay earning him an Oscar nomination.

But neither was a horror film, so both were atypical as King-inspired projects.

The Mist gets back to horror basics, with more generic but less impressive results.

Darabont is, in a way, returning to his roots, having written the scripts for A Nightmare on Elm Street 3: Dream Warriors, The Blob, and The Fly II early on in his career.

But The Mist, in addition to being a monster flick, is also a group-dynamics survival thriller in the vein of TV series like The Twilight Zone and Outer Limits.

A freak storm in rural Maine unleashes a strange species of bloodthirsty creatures from waaa-a-a-ay out of town. The justifiably desperate, petrified townsfolk take refuge from the enveloping wet fog and barricade themselves in the supermarket.

The array of stock characters includes Thomas Jane as the protective father of a young son, Marcia Gay Harden as a religious fanatic who blames the locals’ sins and God’s consequent wrath for their current travail, Andre Braugher as a hard-edged attorney, Toby Jones as the common-sensical assistant store manager with hidden talents, Laurie Holden as the town’s newest and perhaps most attractive citizen, Jeffrey DeMunn as the first shopper to have a close encounter of the bloody kind, and Bill Sadler as a belligerent handyman.

courtesy The Mist official site  

What appear to be trying to get into the store – and not for casual shopping – are extra-terrestrial insects the size of farm equipment, some of which fly, some of which crawl, all of which feed, and all of which kill.

But what they’re feeding on are not Aisle Three snacks. And what they’re killing is not time.

The human cast is generally capable – although Braugher is off his game, stooping to the occasion and never quite owning his dialogue.The confined setting works to the film’s advantage in the building of suspense and inflating of the imagined terror. And, of course, some of that terror doesn’t have to be imagined at all because it’s enacted.

Ultimately, this is a creature feature, after all – courtesy of modest CGI – but it’s a character-driven piece at that. If only the primary characters were less shallow and more compelling.

In the extended middle portion, the film gives itself over far too indulgently to speechifying as the traumatized residents debate, form allegiances, and decide how best to deal with their life-threatening predicament. That the theme of the monster within the people and thus the danger within the store is the script’s main concern is fine, but it gets hammered at too directly and articulated too often.

For folks swept up in the narrative, however, the anything-but-generic ending should deliver quite a shiver. While the rest of us are left wishing the rest of the film operated at quite the same level of thoughtful creativity.

So we’ll stock the supermarket shelves with 2 stars out of 4 for a middling movie adaptation of a Stephen King horror chiller.

One that falls just a few Mist-takes short of being a memorable scare package.
