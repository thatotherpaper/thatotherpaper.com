Sleuth

2 November 2007 | Bill Wine

It doesn’t take a master sleuth to deduce why Sleuth the Second is criminally subpar.

It’s a stylish but inert remake of a memorable cat-and-mouse-game thriller.

courtesy IMDb  

The original 1972 movie version of Sleuth, based on the British play by Anthony Shaffer, was a stimulating, twisty mystery-thriller directed by Joseph L. Mankiewicz and featuring Oscar-nominated performances by the two leads, Laurence Olivier and Michael Caine.

Now, 35 years later, Caine has taken over the role played before by Olivier, while Jude Law (also a producer) has inherited Caine’s role in the first version.

The plot: Andrew Wyke (Caine), a wealthy, successful novelist – nicknamed “The Master of Menace” – invites Milo Tindle (Law), his wife’s lover, an unemployed actor and a much younger man, to the opulent high-tech mansion on his sprawling country estate to meet him.

“What’s it all about?” the second actor to star in Alfie asks the first.

Wyke makes Tindle a proposition: He’ll grant his wife a divorce so that she and her new man can be together if Tindle will pretend to burglarize Wyke’s home. He is to steal a valuable necklace, which he can then fence and thus create financial resources for the anointed couple, while the consequent insurance money will help Wyke maintain his accustomed lifestyle.

You would think there’s a lot more to it than what Wyke explains and you would, of course, be correct. But their witty war of words and worry will take several sharp turns along the way.

Director Kenneth Branagh (Henry V, Dead Again, Much Ado About Nothing, Frankenstein) has directed this new version with elegance and unapologetically larger-than-life theatricality.

But also with a self-consciousness that robs the material – essentially an extravagant parlor trick – of much of its power.

He seems enamored of the sets and props and technology, and he allows them to upstage the performers. These items may add a touch of visual allure, but they end up detracting from the narrative momentum.

The blackly comic screenplay by Harold Pinter – a study of jealousy, power, and class conflict, with utter humiliation the mutual goal – has the two combatants playing ping pong with barbs instead of balls.

But the film is much (almost an hour) shorter than its predecessor and – now here’s an unusual complaint – too brief to get us to feel much in the way of even momentary empathy for either character.

If, then, we at least bought all their actions as believable, perhaps we wouldn’t be bothered by the lack of emotional connection. But that, alas, is not the case.

Whereas the original managed to be playfully edgy and upbeat, the redo is plodding, spasmodic, and downbeat. And the curious third act is a mess, with playwright Pinter becoming uncharacteristically literal.

His narrative pretty much gives up on itself, and, from out of left field – or some other film – the scenarist suddenly drills below ground and lifts a halfhearted homoerotic subtext to the surface.

Oh, so that’s what this is all about.

The dialogue, which should be, and sometimes has been, crackling, suddenly sputters affectlessly.

The film’s one major twist, hardly a surprise at this late date, remains intact, just as unsuccessful – and just as forgivable – as it was in the first version. But it’s just a minor misstep in a middling movie that never replaces your furrowed brow with a satisfied smile. Law is adequate in his role. And Caine is, once again, magnificent, holding the screen with ease and squeezing all the juice possible from every silent stare.

(What might really be fun, or at least more fun, would be to see Micheal Caine in both roles – aging Caine as Andrew and young Caine as Milo – aided, of course, by laser technology to play opposite himself.)

But we exit wondering why they even bothered with this exhumation. So we’ll solve 2 stars out of 4 for a problematic update of the crime caper, Sleuth.

Where all the merry menace and nasty fun has been buried remains a mystery.
