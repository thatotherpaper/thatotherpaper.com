The Golden Compass

7 December 2007 | Bill Wine
The Golden Compass 

You don’t need a compass to tell you that The Golden Compass, anything but golden, has lost its way.

Way to where?, you ask. To the promised land of big-screen franchise trilogies not unlike the spectacularly successful The Lord of the Rings.

Instead, it finds itself stranded on the Isle of Wannabe.

The Golden Compass is a children’s action-adventure fantasy, the movie adaptation of the first children’s book (1995) in Philip Pullman’s best-selling His Dark Materials trilogy, with The Subtle Knife and The Amber Spyglass presumably to follow.

It’s set in an alternate, parallel universe, one in which it’s up to certain youngsters to smite malevolent adult forces.

Pullman being an avowed atheist, his story’s more controversial anti-church elements have been toned down by writer-director Chris Weitz for the family audience.

That is, the narrative certainly questions the absolute authority of organized religion, but without any direct references to particular faiths. This has obviously failed to satisfy certain boycotting Christian groups.

Dakota Blue Richards stars as Lyra Belacqua, an 11-year-old orphan who grows up as a ward of a nearby university.

Daniel Craig plays Lyra’s distracted uncle and guardian, an anthropologist and don at the university, who is off to the Arctic Circle to investigate a mysterious substance called dust, which makes possible travel between universes. No, Lyra can’t come with.

But she wants to, and is given the chance by her new, temporary guardian, one Mrs. Coulter, a world traveler played by Nicole Kidman who seems suspiciously anxious to please, and offers to take Lyra with her.

In this world, science, religion, and magic intertwine: For example, every person is accompanied by a daemon, an animal-shaped manifestation of his or her soul.

And it is a world dominated by the Magisterium, which seeks to control humanity and thus opposes the freedom that dust makes possible through the comparison of alternatives.

Lyra’s impossible mission – and perhaps her destiny – if she chooses to accept it (and how can she not?) is to keep her truth-revealing Alethiometer (check the title) away from Mrs. Coulter and the Magisterium, who want to possess it, and to rescue her best friend from the clutches of the “gobblers,” who use kids for their sinister experiments.

But she soon learns that a trap has been set for her, and that it’s no easy task to identify just who are allies and who are the enemies in a war that has already begun.

Chris Weitz (co-director, with brother Paul, of About a Boy and Down to Earth) thrusts us right into the (suffocatingly) metaphorical adventure, as if guided by the assumption that every viewer has read the book.

The numerous talking, morphing, scene-stealing animals, handled adroitly from a special-effects standpoint, eventually become annoyingly distracting – no matter how much they delight the youngest audience members – and are overdone by half. We may applaud the techno-magic, but we don’t feel much of anything as we do.

With precious little in the way of character development or legitimate emotional involvement, this is one look-but-don’t-touch-or-be-touched adventure.

And, chattering animal life or not, it should be added that this PG-13 attraction is probably too frightening for the real little ones, not so much scary as occasionally startling.

The Golden Compass is fanciful, imaginative, and escapist, but it’s decidedly mirthless and narratively arbitrary, and doesn’t connect with ordinary experience the way, say, the Harry Potter movies do.

Several of the special effects-laden set pieces – the climactic CGI warrior bear battle, for instance – are so elaborate, they make the whole film seem animated.

Rarely has there been a movie that embraced its own incompleteness so unapologetically, that ignored the idea of self-containment so blatantly. By the time we exit The Golden Compass, which is anything but self-contained, it has to come to seem nothing more than the table-setter for the two sequels to come.

And leaving us not caring whether they ever show up or not.

Speaking of truth-telling: Nicole Kidman and Daniel Craig bring marquee value and little more to the table, as both are supporting characters with Kidman having limited screen time and Craig a not-even-glorified cameo.

Maybe they’re to be more prominently featured in the sequel(s).

Anyway, we’ll exorcise our daemons with 2 stars out of 4 for a family fantasy about free will with direction problems: Despite visions of magnetic north, The Golden Compass finds itself quickly heading south.

This film is now playing in theaters
