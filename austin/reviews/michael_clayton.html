Michael Clayton

11 October 2007 | Bill Wine
Related links
  • Michael Clayton official site

He mops up, but he’s not a janitor.

He sells concepts, but he’s not a salesman.

He cleans up messes, but he’s not a sanitation worker.

He’s Michael Clayton. He’s a “fixer.” And he’s in a fix.

courtesy Warner Bros official site 

Michael Clayton is a legal thriller, but not a courtroom drama. Rather, it takes place in the corridors and back alleys of the corporate cocoon.

George Clooney plays the title character, an in-house fixer for Kenner, Bach & Ledeen, one of Manhattan’s largest corporate law firms.

A former criminal prosecutor, he went for the big bucks a few years back and switched sides.He remains skilled at handling damage control on the firm’s behalf. His damaged life, however, is another story.

He’s a divorced, world-weary, frustrated single dad, estranged from his family, deep in debt, addicted to gambling, drenched in self-loathing, strapped by a failing business venture, and deeply involved in a wrestling match with his conscience.

When his firm is hit with a $3-billion class action suit, Marty Bach, the co-founder played by Sydney Pollack, who is attempting to finesse a merger, asks Clayton to look into their boffo case.

It’s a six-year-long poisonous-pollution entanglement involving the agrochemical multinational conglomerate, U/North, which is in the wrong–and thus may be responsible for many deaths – but seems headed for a legal victory.

But then Kenner Bach’s efforts are sabotaged by Arthur Edens, the firm’s chief litigator, a guilt-ridden, bipolar fixer played by the reliable and electrifying Tom Wilkinson, who has gone off his meds while working on a pretrial settlement.

Arthur is so crazy that he – as if acting out the moral dilemma that Michael is trying not to think about – actually starts spouting the truth and threatening to switch sides and help the plaintiffs.

Oh, and he’s also taken to stripping during depositions.

Time for Michael, who’s also his friend, to reel him in.

Meanwhile, U/North’s icy chief counsel, Karen Crowder (Tilda Swinton), chasing a multi-million-dollar settlement as her ticket to super-success, decides in a sweaty panic to stop Arthur in his tracks in ways that do not involve conversation.

Time for Arthur to look the other way. If he can.

courtesy Warner Bros official site 

Writer-director Tony Gilroy – who scripted the fine Bourne trilogy, as well as The Devil’s Advocate and Proof of Life – makes his directorial debut in this disturbing slice of greed-is-good corporate life.

His dense, demanding, idiosyncratic script–layered (perhaps to a fault) with flashforwards and flashbacks over a four-day stretch, a non-linear time-line that is not easy to follow – is complex and morally ambiguous, even if the slow-build early reels fail to eventuate to a tingly level of suspense, a few plot contrivances gnaw at us, several plot strands wave in the breeze, and we never stop wishing for a bit more narrative momentum.

But the dialogue is intelligent and authentic all the way through. And the acting is crackerjack – from Clooney, who dials down the charm quotient just a notch, through Wilkinson, Swinton, and Pollack – as Gilroy makes a smooth transition from the screenwriter’s keyboard to the director’s chair.

Reminiscent of Erin Brockovich, which similarly addressed corporate malfeasance, and The Verdict, which featured Paul Newman as a failed lawyer on the comeback trail, Michael Clayton is a morality tale about legality as well as a character study about a character with character.

So we’ll litigate 3 stars out of 4 for a thoughtful and piercing jurisprudential drama.

Michael Clayton settles unsettlingly out of court.

Micheal Clayton opens nationwide on October 12
