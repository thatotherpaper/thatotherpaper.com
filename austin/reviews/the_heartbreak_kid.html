The Heartbreak Kid

4 October 2007 | Bill Wine
courtesy Heartbreak Kid official site Ben Stiller in The Heartbreak Kid 
Related links
  • Heartbreak Kid official site

The honeymoon ends quickly in The Heartbreak Kid – for the characters and for us.

This major misfire by the Farrelly Brothers is a remake of a memorable dark comedy, only tweaked and updated and transplanted and de-ethnicized, and thus refashioned it in their raunchy, slapsticky style.

Ben Stiller stars as 40-ish Eddie Cantrow, owner of a San Francisco sporting goods store.

courtesy Heartbreak Kid official site The Heartbreak Kid 

Afraid that this might be his last chance to find romantic happiness, he proposes to the striking and sexually alluring Lisa (Cameron Diaz-resembling Malin Akerman), who seems too good to be true, after knowing and dating her for only a few weeks.

It turns out that Lisa’s anything but good or true, at least in Eddie’s wandering eyes.

On their honeymoon at a Mexican resort, she reveals herself to be remarkably crazed, littering their bridal suite with offputting idiosyncracies and upsetting, flabbergasting secrets.

Simultaneously, Eddie meets another hotel guest, the sweet, down-to-earth Miranda, played by Michelle Monaghan, whom he quickly pegs as his true soulmate.

Yep, no matter how you look at it, Eddie has made a HUGE mistake.

Whereupon he sets out to pursue and woo Miranda, keep it from Lila (who is confined to their hotel room with a cartoonishly severe case of sunburn), hide his marital status from Miranda, and win over her skeptical Southern family.

Yep, he’s trying to take The Courtship of Eddie’s Farther.

The indelible 1972 original Heartbreak Kid was scripted by Neil Simon, based on a short story (A Change of Plan) by Bruce Jay Friedman, directed by Elaine May, and starred Charles Grodin and Cybill Shepherd.

This version, co-directed by Bobby and Peter Farrelly (There’s Something About Mary, Dumb & Dumber, Shallow Hal, Fever Pitch), has been rejiggered: Whereas in the original the husband ditches his plain wife for a younger, flashier blonde, this time he turns from his looker of a sexually voracious wife to an appealing, wholesome brunette.

Originally developed as a vehicle for Jason Bateman and Amy Poehler, Heartbreak Deux was first titled Seven Day Itch.

Well, it’s itchy, all right. Twitchy too. But it’s also scattered (five credited screenwriters), battered (seems like at least three different movies) and shattered (a less approved-of protagonist you’ll never find).

As the Farrellys have done in many of their worst films, they offer a collection of envelope-pushing moments of comic outrageousness – vulgarian sight gags that feel at the time of each like the film’s raison d’etre.

courtesy Heartbreak Kid official site The Heartbreak Kid 

The problem is that they’re obviously and significantly less funny than their creators feel they are – so much so, that you feel sorry for the performers forced to enact them.

These intended-to-be-talked-about “highlights” don’t so much punctuate the narrative as subvert and distract from it.

Not that the story is any great shakes: The film changes tone several times, as if breaking down into a collection of shorter features.

The dialogue is better than the story deserves and the comic timing on display is at least respectable.

But Ben Stiller has an uphill slog, playing a monumentally unsympathetic protagonist.

And the supporting cast – including Jerry Stiller, Ben’s real-life dad, as his dad, and Rob Corddry as his best friend – is serviceable.

But the script does them all in, coming apart in the late stages to such a degree, you find yourself longing for the originality and professionalism of the original. This one is a candidate for inclusion in the platoon of Unnecessary and Wrongheaded Remakes.

So we’ll elope with 2 stars out of 4 for a forced and fumbling screwball romp from the Farrelly Brothers, the gross-out romance, The Heartbreak Kid.

We may not demand an annulment, but we are certainly, like the protagonist, divorced from any real attachment.
