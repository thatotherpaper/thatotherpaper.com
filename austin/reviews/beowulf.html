Beowulf

15 November 2007 | Bill Wine
courtesy Beowulf official site  
This isn’t your father’s Beowulf. Or your grandfather’s. But it just might be your son’s. Or your daughter’s.

Beowulf is a CGI-animated action-adventure epic based on the Old English poem you hated reading in high school.

Well, the ancient poem has been yanked into the modern era by innovative director Robert Zemeckis, who has never been one to duck out of the way of a challenge. After all, his filmography includes such impressive groundbreakers as the Back to the Future trilogy, Who Framed Roger Rabbit, Death Becomes Her, Forrest Gump (which brought him the Oscar for Best Director), Contact, Cast Away, and The Polar Express. With Beowulf, he accepts, or perhaps seeks out, another moviemaking dare.

To wit: Is it possible to turn a seemingly impenetrable Anglo-Saxon alliterative verse poem written over a thousand years ago – and, as perhaps the oldest tale in the English language, the not-so-fondly-recalled bane of many a high schooler’s existence – into a modern mainstream movie using the motion-capture technology that transforms live action into digital animation?

Hey, sure, why not?

And in the form of an animation-stylized PG-13 attraction for the masses that would, if it were live action, certainly be R-rated.

Set in the legendary time of heroes and monsters in Denmark, at the beginning of the sixth century, Beowulf is about a Scandinavian warrior, the title character voiced by Ray Winstone, who lives in Herot, a land ruled by hedonistic King Hrothgar (Anthony Hopkins) and Queen Wealthow (Robin Wright Penn). When the hideous and tormented demon, Grendel (Crispin Glover), threatens the people, Beowulf volunteers to kill him. Which he does, leading to a grateful and heir-less King Hrothgar (Anthony Hopkins) announcing Beowulf’s succession to the throne.

courtesy Beowulf official site  

But Grendel’s vengeance-seeking mother, seductively voiced by Angelina Jolie, encounters and propositions the desirous Beowulf, who is also pretty much consumed by his intense desire for wealth, power and fame. And, years later, Beowulf will have to face a fierce dragon that once again threatens to kill everyone in its path.

Beowulf is being released simultaneously in 2-D and 3-D (which it was really fashioned for), and in IMAX. Not that you have to see it in 3-D Imax, but it is, in that format, one eye-popping marvel.

And our usual complaint about 3-D – that it’s a gimmick that makes short shrift of the narrative and other production values – does not apply.

Zemeckis makes sure of that, and seems to be pointing the way to the future – or, at least, one of the futures – of the movie experience.

There is something bizarrely fascinating about watching digital replicas of familiar actors, who in this case also include John Malkovich as Beowulf’s nemesis, Brendan Gleeson as his sidekick, and Alison Lohman as a young love of the old king.

The screenplay by Neil Gaiman and Roger Avary, who have taken thoughtful and appropriate liberties with the source material, offers Beowulf not as a superhero but as a seriously flawed antihero, and touches on the twin themes of regret and redemption.

But, despite a dollop of purposeful playfulness and humor – and Zemeckis is not shy about being campy – one problem that undercuts the screenplay’s ambitions is the amount of unintentionally hilarious moments or exchanges. Far too often for any kind of comfort, Beowulf seems a parody of itself. But we forgive it its trespasses.

The motion-capture process, however, has been substantially improved since 2004’s The Polar Express, especially in the area of facial expressiveness, which means that motion capture now does a better job of emotion capture.

And the climax is a thrilling, brilliantly designed and executed battle.

Ancient poetry meets modern technology in Beowulf and the result is an immersive experience worth an exhilirating 3 stars out of 4.

Beowulf just may be a monster at the box office.

This movie opens nationwide on November 16
