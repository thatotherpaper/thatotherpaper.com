The Great Blizzard of 2007

7 February 2007 | Todd Ross Nienkerk

On the afternoon of Tuesday, January 16, the staff of That Other Paper awoke to a startling sight: 1/16 inch of ice and snow had blanketed Austin like a tissue-thin glaze. We were completely snowed in, and only the mild, lukewarm breath of an asthmatic infant could free us from our icy prisons.

This is the true story of one man’s survival against all odds.

Day one: Hell on ice

I awoke around noon to a disturbing sensation: A slight chill had entered the room, rendering me temporarily uncomfortable. I desperately groped for the comforter, which I’d kicked off during a particularly average dream involving light, recreational jogging and confidently speaking in front of a large group of people with all of my clothes on. I grasped a corner of the sheet and pulled it towards my chin. The warmth soothed me, and I promptly feel asleep, blissfully unaware of the anarchy raging outside my frosted window.

photo / I am K.E.B. Ice Storm ’07 

My alarm rang at 3:00pm.

“Time to begin the day!” I cheered.

I sprang from the bed, refreshed and alert, and charged through the living room and towards the balcony door. With a deft sweep of my rippling arm, I pulled back the vertical blinds to reveal–

“The horror!” I cried.

A thin layer of murderous ice coated the balcony, and monstrous, one-inch-long icicles hung from the railing like Swiss Army Knives of Damocles.

I took a step back in shock, then bolted towards the front door. I gripped the knob, and with massive pecs writhing and popping beneath my silk pajamas, I threw the door open.

The steps in front of my door were frozen over – a treacherous, impassable terrain that would’ve shaken the resolve of Hannibal’s army.

The television confirmed my worst fears: Austin was facing an ice storm unlike the world had ever seen. Central Texas had transformed, overnight, into a desolate, frozen wasteland. Suddenly I regretted turning down that trip to a Siberian death camp – I would’ve stood at least a remote chance of survival.

I quickly assessed the the situation. Did I have enough food? I hadn’t been to Costco in more than two days, so I only had three, maybe four weeks of food in the house. Would it be enough? I would have to ration myself to no more than two Atlantic salmon steaks, five turkey burgers, and four pounds of organic, whole-wheat pasta per day. It seemed impossible, I know, but the human body can withstand a great deal of deprivation when survival is at stake.

My next concern was maintaining my sanity. Cabin fever, after all, is the second biggest killer of those subjected to prolonged, wintry isolation – right behind suffocation by flannel.

I had to keep my mind occupied. Digital cable with HBO On Demand and a ten-hour backlog of recorded television on DVR? That wouldn’t do. That bookshelf filled with literally hundreds of books I’ve been meaning to read but never had the time? Nah. Utilizing the limitless possibility of my imagination? Pffft.

After much deliberation and a little bit of crying, I settled on counting the threads in my Egyptian cotton bedsheets. By the time I reached 52,000 threads and finished Everybody Loves Raymond season two on DVD, the sun had set over the frozen hell that was once Hill Country. With nothing but my laptop and fully functioning central heating to keep me warm, I hunkered down for a long, desperate night.

As I drifted into a watchful sleep, I silently prayed that the frozen souls of the damned would not wake me with their shrill screams of retribution. I also prayed that the Saints would take it all the way and that I’d find a million dollars in a paper sack behind the bed while having sex with, like, twenty hot chicks.

Day two: God obviously hates us or something

The moment I awoke, I flipped on the television. The local news stations were abuzz with what they had dubbed “Ice Storm ’07.” That they dared downplay the severity of this crisis was truly shocking. This is no simple ice storm, I thought. This is the wrath of an angry God – a precursor to Armageddon.

As KXAN gleefully tallied traffic accidents, I considered the plight of the masses below. From my elevated position on the third floor of an apartment building at the top of a hill, I was protected from attack on all sides. But mere miles away, Austinites were battling to the death along Congress Avenue. Mothers, insane from hunger, were prying food from the mouths of their children; beloved family pets were being torn apart and devoured raw as arterial blood spurted from their gaping chests.

I noticed a rumbling in my stomach. Hunger was overpowering me, too – though I wasn’t craving the twitching, steamy flesh of the fallen. Flaxseed and pomegranate waffles will do just fine, I thought as I browsed my overflowing freezer.

I toasted and devoured my breakfast, savoring each nourishing morsel while trying not to face the harrowing reality: I was down to only three dozen waffles, and resupply was tens of hours away.

As I licked the plate, a tremendous noise rose from the parking lot below – a deathly roar followed by a high-pitched squeal. The seven trumpets of the angels! I thought. The reign of the beast is nigh!

I dropped my plate and bounded towards the door. Outside, I discovered the angels had not unleashed a consuming hail of fire and blood upon the Earth. One of my neighbors was attempting to back out of a parking space.

photo / I am K.E.B. Ice Storm ’07 

“You fool!” I cried.

Alas, the doomed soul couldn’t hear me above the revving engine and squealing tires. I crouched to lower my center of gravity and inched towards the icy railing.

“Hark!” I called through cupped hands. “I bid unto you, heed my warning! ’Tis a fool’s errand to commit such torque upon a frictionless surfa–”

His front tire caught an exposed patch of pavement, wrenching his vehicle sideways and backwards. I watched in horror as he slammed his brakes – an obvious mistake made only by those with little experience driving in icy conditions – sending his car gliding down the hill at speeds topping inches per minute.

“I beg of you! Release your hold upon the braking pedal and steer – steer into the drift!”

But it was all too late. I watched, helpless, as his car approached the rear bumper of a parked truck. I tightened my grip on the railing and braced for impact.

The car exploded on contact, splattering flaming bits of rubber, upholstery, and gore across the sides of the buildings. Twelve charred, headless children carrying machine guns climbed out of the burning hulk like angry ants whose hill has just been poked with a dirty chopstick. They began firing randomly in all directions.

A bullet whizzed past my ear, shattering the wood panel next to my door. I ducked, dove into my apartment, and fastened that little chain-lock thing.

Inside, I grabbed a mid-priced bottle of Grenache by the neck and smashed it against the edge of the counter. Facing the door, I stood with my crude weapon at the ready. My huge, masculine fists trembled in anticipation of plunging broken glass deep into the chest of gun-wielding zombie children and hearing their damned souls escape their wounds like a boiling kettle.

Then I heard a voice from behind: “I’m gonna punch your face off!”

I turned in place with the speed of a glacier wading through waist-high mud in slow-motion.

“Krrggtt!” I cried. It was the Icy Cat of Death.

This is the Icy Cat of Death 

“I– I thought you were only a myth!”

The Icy Cat of Death reared up on its hinds legs and unleashed a bone-rattling laugh. Its hellish breath smelled like fish and old socks.

“I am no myth,” it bellowed. “I am borne of the plight of man. I am created in the darkness between silent screams. I have been summoned to this time of crisis – to this epic ice storm – by the cries of the mildly inconvenienced.”

I inched backwards as the Icy Cat of Death approached. Panicked, I poked the broken bottle in his direction, but he swiped it away with a gigantic paw.

I was sure I’d met my end. Eaten alive by a magical, demonic cat in the middle of an ice storm in Central Texas – I mean, how cliché is that?

The Icy Cat of Death reared up and howled. In an instant, he descended upon me, plucking me from the ground in his massive jaws. With a flick of his head, he flipped me into the air and swallowed me whole. My vision went dark, and the world fell silent.

Day three: The thaw

I spent the morning listening to the ice melt and reflecting. The madness had consumed me. A full day and a half of isolation had stretched my sanity to the breaking point. I’m amazed I recovered so quickly. Hell, I’m amazed I survived. I was one of the lucky ones.

No one will ever know how many Austinites froze, starved, or went insane during Ice Storm ’07. Seriously, don’t even bother trying to count or investigate it any further. Let’s just all agree that it was the worst natural disaster in history, and it deserved every moment of sweaty, frenetic media attention it received.

So what did I learn from this experience? I learned that global warming is a hoax. I mean, c’mon – did you see how totally cold it got? Once again, the facts demonstrate that “scientists” don’t actually do anything. They just make stuff up to win government grants and get brain implants.

I also learned that KXAN’s coverage of Ice Storm ’07 was vastly superior to other local news stations’. News 8 and KEYE should be embarrassed. They suck so hard at covering ice storms. I hope everyone there gets fired. Then I hope they eat bad oysters and throw up for, like, a millennium. Then I hope they slip in the throw-up.

So anyway, that’s my story. To summarize, I’m a badass, and KXAN is the number-one station for “Ice Storm ’07” coverage – here’s proof. Oh, and in case you’re wondering, Katz’s didn’t klose.
