Opening presence

15 November 2006 | Adam Avramescu
Related links
  • Fishboy’s website
  • Fishboy’s MySpace page

Saturday, September 30, at 11:30pm: Spoon takes the stage at another sold-out Emo’s show. The crowd – one fluid mass of tattooed and pierced skin, cigarette smoke, and American Apparel – shrieks collectively the moment Britt Daniel, all swagger and Tecate and fitted shirt, approaches the mic.

photo / Chad Wadsworth 

I hear a bouncer mutter to a bartender: “Seventy-five of these schmucks are on Spoon’s guest list.” It’s a triumphant return home for Austin’s indie flagship, and they won’t disappoint. From the opening strains of “My Mathematical Mind” to the jam out from “Sister Jack,” the band managed to run through most of their recent – and by recent, I mean the past six years – material, leaving at least a third of the crowd waiting hours for that turn my camera on song. (What, they haven’t seen a Jaguar commercial lately?)

The other two-thirds hang on Britt’s every word, the band’s every note. The packed room pulses with energy.

Earlier that night, around 9:30pm, the scene looked much different. The crowd – the diehard fans willing to suffer through the opening acts for an up-close look at the set list, or what brand of sticks Jim Eno uses, or Britt’s crotch, or something – was only three or four rows thick. That’s when my band Fishboy took the stage.

As exciting as it was to play at the place where I saw so many of my favorite shows, we had our reservations. We had opened for Spoon in Dallas just before they released Gimme Fiction. That show was at Trees – think Stubb’s big stage, but indoors – and the results were less than optimal. You see, the Dallas Hipster is a different breed than we’re used to. Dallas is one of those towns that hasn’t gotten the memo about metal – the one that says it’s culturally irrelevant – and it’s left a dent on the indie scene. Neglected and reliant on touring bands, the indie kids are snobbier than we’re used to. And better dressed.

Needless to say, the only hearts we won over that night were the high-school kids who had heard of Spoon from The O.C. The “these guys will change your life” set. Those kids like everything.

Fearing a reprise of Dallas, and wanting to pad out our set time so more people could see us, our lead singer advised us to “lie low and let the set build.” So we took the stage at the biggest little indie club in another town that was probably too cool for us, receiving a mix of encouraging shouts from our friends who had come to see us and pity calls from the sparse group of Spoon diehards camping out in front of the stage. Damned if we weren’t going to win them over.

We were surprised to find a crowd that actually got into it. Maybe because they were younger than the average Dallas crowd. Maybe because they had gone the whole week without listening to any music. Maybe because they had secretly donned earbuds and were listening to Franz Ferdinand while we played, they were actually moving around and cheering. I’ve been on the other side of the stage, and I can tell a genuine response from a canned one. I’d like to say we got a bit of the former.

photo / Chad Wadsworth 

By the time we reached our rousing finale, there were perhaps 100 people in the club. A fraction of what the crowd would be, but not a bad number for a D-list indie band trying to get its footing in a new city. And hearing the faint cheering of that crowd, I felt just as triumphant about performing as Spoon would later that night. It hit me that this was the very stage where the New Pornographers played during my first week of college. This was the very spot where Richard Reed Parry from the Arcade Fire knocked over a cymbal in a fit of musical passion, nearly slicing me in two. I looked back to the door backstage and saw Britt Daniel hiding in the doorway. He cracked a smile of approval.

Later, Britt would try to console us. “I wish they had let you go on a little later,” he said. “Might’ve been more people.” But I didn’t need any consolation. I mean, come on – we opened for frickin’ Spoon.

About the author Adam Avramescu spent last summer touring with his band, Fishboy. In his spare time, he is an English and Rhetoric and Writing senior at UT-Austin. This is Adam’s first contribution to That Other Paper.
