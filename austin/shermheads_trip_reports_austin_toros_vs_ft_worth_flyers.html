Shermhead’s trip reports: Austin Toros vs. Fort Worth Flyers

30 November 2006 | Aaron Stanush and Chris Andrews
photo / Jeff Kramer 

I have never been a fan of competitive sports: I don’t agree with mandatory drug testing, and years of low-grade methamphetamine abuse have deteriorated my vestibular nerve to the point where stadium acoustics give me vertigo and cause me to vomit uncontrollably. I was given a ticket to see the Toros play Fort Worth by a customer in lieu of a tip for a pizza delivery last week, though, and I figured I’d give it a chance. Having spent all weekend watching the same 90-minute anime on loop while on a collection of psychotropic drugs, I was ready to change my sweat-soaked hoodie and leave my one-room apartment.

I remembered people in high school saying that they went to basketball games drunk. The Toros are in the NBA development league, which I guess is a step below the NBA. Since the game would be more intense than a high-school match, I decided that the best way to witness the event was on three ounces of PCP, a half-quart of elephant tranquilizers, and a Cheetos box full of wild Mexican peyote. I don’t like to leave the house without chemical assistance; real life is way too surreal to experience as-is. Besides, beers run like $5 each at the stadium, and a pizza delivery guy has to cut corners where he can. My drug dependence is really just home economics.

The Austin Convention Center was a zoo by the time I got there. I stowed my bike in some bushes on 1st Street and hurried through the ticket gate, but it took me what felt like years to find my seat – I kept circling the convention center hallways, watching the seasons change and the landscape evolve from arid plains to verdant pastures to barren, lifeless expanses of charred rock. After the apocalypse, only the three master species of the six races of splendor remained: The Night Elves, the Elder Daemons, and the Humans. Luckily, I was seeing a lot of my human kin in the stadium.

A friendly Daemon police officer stopped me after a while and offered to help me find my seat:

“To which overlord are you loyal, mortal?”

The roar of the crowd drowned out the officer’s voice, so I asked him to repeat himself.

“Are you okay, son? Where is your ticket?”

I gave him my ticket and he pointed me in the direction of my seat. I ended up being right in the front, only three rows up from the players’ bench and next to a family of five. At this point, the Flyers were up by six with 21 minutes left in the second half. I noticed that Brad Buckman, former UT forward, was playing for the Toros and had grown a third arm. I guess he developed that after graduation.

The Flyers dominated the court in the second half, keeping the Toros on their heels and the crowd subdued. I tried ordering a pretzel at one point, but just as I was handing the vendor my money the referee blew his whistle, shooting shrapnel in every direction of the stadium. I screamed in terror, tackled the vendor the protect him, and covered my head to shield it from the metal shards raining down from the sky. I realized after getting to my feet that my entire section of crowd was silent and horrified – the ref must have made a terrible call. I shouted obscenities toward the floor and made my way to my seat.

I was more interested in the enormous spider crafting a web at the top of the stadium than the game at this point. The Toros were clearly not giving the Flyers 100%, and a turnaround seemed impossible by the last few minutes. I decided to leave early to beat the traffic and started for the exit, but the escalator alongside the bleachers was still moving down. I had to fight my way up the stands inch-by-inch, and the game was over by the time I made it to the top. The Toros lost, 111-96.

Already disappointed in the Toros’ loss, I became furious when I had to barter with the bushes outside the stadium for my bike. Even if I had three vials of dragon’s blood, I shouldn’t have to pay to park at a sporting event. Luckily a crowd gathered around the scene and began pointing at the opportunistic shrubs, calling them “fucking insane” and “that crazy asshole who tackled the vendor during the game.” Embarrassed, they gave me my bike and I headed home.

I’m not sure what to think of the Toros. Sure, having a professional basketball team in town is exciting, but college sports will always reign king in Austin. With NBA teams in Dallas, Houston, and San Antonio, it’s hard to argue that Austinites don’t have a pro basketball franchise to rally behind. Who knows, though – I may just be upset because I was chased by a bloodthirsty serpent over my entire ride home.
