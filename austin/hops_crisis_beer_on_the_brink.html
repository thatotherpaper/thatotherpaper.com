Hops crisis: Beer on the brink

18 February 2008 | Michael Pool
photo / suzanneandsimon Referred to as hop vine, hops is actually a bine 

Ten years ago some farmers couldn’t give hops away to beer brewers. Several years of overproduction had left the hops market saturated with supply and short on buyers. Today that couldn’t be further from the truth. Over the next few years beer enthusiasts around the world may be feeling the pinch on both their palates and their pocketbooks.

Hops and barley shortages

Hops are to the brewing process what spices are to cooking. Each variety of hops provides its own unique flavors when added into a brew, and each particular beer recipe is fairly dependent on the varieties and amounts of hops it uses. Craft brewers in particular use much higher amounts of hops in their brewing process in order to achieve the complex, “hoppy” flavor in beers such as India Pale Ales (IPAs). Consequently, small craft brewers may feel the effects of the shortage the hardest of anyone within the brewing industry in the coming months or years.

As the supply of hops peaked out in the late 1990s, value bottomed out. In response to this loss of value, many farmers around the world lowered their acreage of hops in favor of other crops that brought better prices. Some even stopped farming hops altogether over the next several years. There was no collective approach within the industry to address the decline in the value of hops. This apparent miscommunication led to an excessive number of farmers switching to other crops and thus, a decade later, a shortage. The lower-yielding hops varieties (and often the more aromatic) have especially seen their supply dry up to a trickle in recent months.

The effects of the hops shortage are just starting to manifest now in early 2008. The cost of hops has as much as tripled for some varieties in as many months. In response, many breweries – including giants like Anheuser Busch (brewer of Budweiser, Michelob, Busch, and many seasonal/specialty beers) – are raising prices to combat the resulting profit cuts.

While the hops shortage alone might not have been enough to merit raising prices for some breweries, as the old saying goes: Timing is everything. In this case the timing of the shortage couldn’t have been worse.

photo / metoc Barley is an annual cereal grain 

Barley, the main ingredient in most beer, has also seen a huge surge in cost due to a supply shortage recently. Drought in Australia and weather problems in Europe have resulted in poor barley harvests in those regions two years running. This compounded with the rise of ethanol – and subsequent increase in the value of the corn used to make it – caused many farmers to switch to farming corn rather than barley; it also caused some ranchers to ditch corn in favor of barley-based feed. This simultaneous drop in supply and rise in demand caused the value of barley to rise.

At least for the immediate future the supply of barley is not threatened to the point of crisis, and most brewers will still be able to get the barley they need – they’ll just have to pay more for it. But when the additional cost of barley is compounded by already skyrocketed hops prices, final product price increases seem inevitable.

The immediate future is a bit more dire for hops than barley. Established microbreweries with future supply contracts should be able to get most of their main varieties of hops. Many specialty hops, however, may become unavailable soon, regardless of price. Those newer, less-established microbreweries and brewpubs (and especially homebrewers) may be out of luck altogether if they don’t already have supply contracts.

Large breweries like Coors and Anheuser Busch will be the most protected from the hops shortage, due to their immense buying power, extensive future supply contracts, and because the American-style pilseners they brew (Budweiser, Bud Light, Coors original, etc.) use much smaller amounts of hops in their recipes.

photo / The Mad Penguin Hop umbel with hopgarden in the background 

Poised for expansion

The hops shortage and rise in barley costs are poor timing for beer sales as well. In 2006, approximately 210 million barrels of beer were brewed in the United states, valued at about $94 billion in sales. The numbers for late 2007 aren’t quite tallied yet, but in the first half alone beer sales increased by about one million barrels compared to that same period in 2006, meaning beer made a resurgence as America’s adult beverage of choice last year.

We’re not just talking about a resurgence in American-style pilsners either. Craft brews have moved to a record share of that market as well, expanding their share by eleven percent in the first half of 2007, and surpassing for the first time ever a five percent dollar share of total beer sales. According to the Brewers Association 400,000 of the one million additional barrels of beer sold in the first half of 2007 were craft beers.

Both the variety and number of brewers seem poised for expansion. Currently the Brewers Association estimates that there are 1,442 breweries in America. The Great American Beer Festival, held annually in Denver, Colorado, had a record number of entries in October 2007 – and many craft brewers around the country found their operations growing, some by as much as eighty percent.

The numbers seem to indicate that craft brewers are coming into their own in the American beer market. But with the supply problems they’re facing, it’s likely that some of that momentum will slow down in 2008.

The unpredictable future

As of now an international scramble for hops is on – brewpubs, homebrewers, and homebrew suppliers are making the rounds, attempting to buy whatever hops they can find. Some homebrew stores are already rationing hops to customers, or making them sign up for waiting lists to get a few ounces of hops as they become available. Speculation abounds within the industry as to whether some breweries will have to close up shop entirely.

One exciting effect of the shortage will be in seeing how the lack of specific varieties of hops will drive experimentation as brewers are forced to alter old recipes or put them on the back burner altogether until the hops they need become available again.

So why not just grow more hops? It takes roughly three years to grow a plot of hops to its first harvest. Consequently, many industry analysts expect the hops shortage to continue in some form until 2011 at least. This means beer enthusiasts everywhere will have to embrace higher prices for the beers they love, and possibly endure a few slight changes in the flavors of their favorite selections. They may even see a few entirely new varieties of beer created with whatever combinations of supplies can be scraped up.

As for homebrewers, some might find themselves leaving the brewing to the professionals for bit, until they can find hops again.

Eventually the hops supply problem should balance itself out as the higher value of hops leads farmers back into the market. Whether too many will return and the cycle will start all over in the next decade, no one knows.

There is some optimism, however, to be had in the near future. It’s possible that many medium-sized and large brewers may have purchased more hops than they needed – just to be safe. As the year plays out and the frenzy settles a bit, those who find they have too much supply may begin to release some of it back into the market. The shortage may have been slightly exaggerated by this hoarding effect, but it’s unlikely that the amount of hops that make their way back onto the market will act as anything more than a band-aid.

In the meantime, beer drinkers will probably have to get used to slightly higher prices and a shift in the varieties of beer on the shelves.

But in so far as the shortage will drive innovation in the brewing industry – this might not be such a bad thing.
