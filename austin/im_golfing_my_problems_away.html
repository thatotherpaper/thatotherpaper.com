I’m golfing my problems away

6 December 2006 | Eric Seufert and Thomas Oltman
photo / Sean Biehle 

Men throughout the ages have always pursued ways to reduce their stress. The cavemen drew crude yet tasteful images of wildlife on the walls of their dwellings; the British played croquet while starting wars and colonizing continents; the Mayans committed ritual sacrifice and cultivated cocaine. Despite carrying the weight of the world on their shoulders, the mighty men of generations past have always enjoyed diversions from the pressure of their hectic lives.

So as I stand here, witnessing the sun retire cozily into the azure sky above the 18th hole at the local course, I have to thank the pasttime gods for getting me through a low point in my life by granting me the love of the greatest game in the world.

Great chip shot, Phil. Get in the hole!

However, looking back, things weren’t always so rosy. Five years ago, my life was a train wreck. Actually, this metaphor should be expanded: my life was a derailed demon train suspended over a sulfurous, all-consuming inferno by the merest of threads. My wife had just left me for my sister, two of my kids were awaiting judgment in their human trafficking trials, and I was earning a meager 80K a year plus quarterly bonuses. My life was in shambles, and the worst part was, aside from hot-boxing the crawl space under my condo before Yes Dear, I had no real hobby.

My pops used to say that every man needs a hobby, and even though his consisted of nervous bouts of crying while talking to the television, that sage piece of wisdom has always reverberated in the mahogany-adorned corridors of my mind.

It wasn’t until I happened to catch the Kevin Costner/Cheech Marin comedy vehicle Tin Cup on TBS while stuck in my living room after an unfortunate Viagra and Ambien overdose that I realized the answer to all my problems: Golf!

Now some might paint me as a clichéd sap on the verge of a predictable mid-life crisis, but this is incontestably false. My stately head is still crowned by a thick, resplendent mane (although I occasionally employ the assistance of Just For Men), and I still drive the same Audi TT I purchased four years ago after my daughter attempted to fake her own death by sending my Suburban into the bottom of a ravine.

But I digress.

Golf is glorious stress relief in a member-restricted, minimum-income-enforced paradise. When I’m out on those emerald links, dancing the tango with only a three-wood and a four-hundred yard fairway as my partner, I forget all about the burdens of meeting pre-determined return standards within a risk-averse investment atmosphere. Sure, a thirty-foot chip shot for birdie can be stressful, but not nearly as stressful as remaining politically correct in an ethnically and culturally diverse workplace. And while my handicap might not be as low as I like, it’s definitely not as low as my self-esteem after being unable to urinate in the public stalls at my office.

Uh oh, here comes that blonde cart girl again! Anyone want a fresh beer? Peterson, you good?

The greatest aspect of golf is the sense of identity it provides in a community where isolation and restraining orders run rampant. Aside from a few awkward stints with online dating, my social life before golf consisted solely of after-office get togethers and the mandatory Narcotics Anonymous meetings I attend bi-weekly at the local Pentecostal church.

Eighteen holes at Pebble Beach have shown me that Eharmony.com and half-a-dozen tweekers could never provide the sense of belonging I feel on the golf course. And although I often find it difficult to understand my ambiguously ethnic caddy’s words through his thick accent, it sure beats superficial conversations with my supervisor David and his partner Dwayne over a plate of fried onions at Chili’s.

Good ball, Randall! Oh no, that one’s wet. Might wanna put on your swimsuit.

Life is short, right? So why not spend your downtime doing something you love. And while I still enjoy the challenge of soliciting senior citizens for thousands of dollars in stock purchases over the phone, I now also enjoy the new challenge of getting out of a bunker in one – ok, two! – shots.
