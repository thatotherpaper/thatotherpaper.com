Emoticons have no place in a professional setting

22 November 2006 | Eric Seufert and Neil Jorgensen
photo / Dan Barbus 

As Project Manager III within Accounts Receivable, I have an acute ability to spot distractions meandering their way into the office. In the ’80s it was the male earring; in the ’90s it was the Walkman and, subsequently, the Discman; and now, in the 21st century, it is the emoticon. Emoticons are an unwelcome introduction of merriment and casual levity into the professional setting, and their use is a threat to the productivity of The Company.

How am I supposed to make numbers (professional slang for meeting pre-determined productivity standards within a defined amount of time) if every email I receive is written in a cryptic Internet code of smiley faces and crudely-represented pieces of the human anatomy? I became so flustered after reading an email littered with emoticons last week that I confused debits for credits in an inventory report and lost The Company over two million dollars. Believe it or not, emoticons affect our bottom line.

Don’t think I’m having an easy time with this decision, either. I’ve worked very hard to divorce myself from the image of a suit-wearing, desperately aloof middle manager. In fact, all of my subordinates think I’m a neat guy. I feel strongly enough on this issue, however, that I’ve drawn a line in the sand: Either you stop using emoticons in office email, or you will receive a documented, verbal recognition of performance deficiency from me. That’s my management style – approachable yet unflinchingly stern.

Emoticons create a dangerous context within written correspondence that opens the contents up to misinterpretation. My attractive secretary sent me an email two weeks ago requesting that I “have a great weekend,” ending the note with a winking smiling face. In face-to-face conversation, such a wink would send a strong signal to the other person that sexual tension was fermenting. You can imagine my surprise, then, when my secretary rebuffed my advances and assailed my professionalism upon our crossing paths at Fuddruckers the following night. Her emoticon use and terrible judgment when faced with an extraordinary opportunity forced me to fire her the next Monday. The reason? Gross incompetence.

I hate firing employees – it requires a lot of paperwork and creates the potential for violent retaliation. If I had my way, employees would never be fired – their keycards would simply be deactivated, and they’d receive a pre-recorded dismissal voicemail. Emoticon use is a threat to The Company’s existence, though, and it therefore warrants such drastic actions. I ran into a similar problem with an employee who habitually wore shorts on Casual Fridays. I fired that reckless bastard and never looked back.

Emoticons are simply unnecessary within the office. The only appropriate opportunity to use a smiley-face would be after the satisfactory completion of an assignment, and I’ve been very diligent in keeping anyone from thinking that has ever been the case. Alternatively, an employee would only be distraught enough to use a frowny-face if his workload were too light; the fact that I’m having my entire group work this weekend should prevent any employees from having the blues over that. No, emoticons are superfluous expressions of office-inappropriate sentiment, and they’ll no longer be tolerated on The Company’s computers.

In the end, I’ve got to look out for my own professional well-being. I don’t know any Project Manager IVs or, fingers crossed, Project Directors who got to where they’re at by letting their inferiors run wild throughout the office. If the fact that my car has not been vandalized in over three weeks is any indication, my employees respect and, more importantly, fear me, which is an office climate that’s sure to boost efficiency while dispelling everyone’s hope of ever rising above my authority. Emoticons can only work to lighten the mood in an otherwise dreary and optimism-suppressing atmosphere, and I’ll be damned if that will happen on my watch.
