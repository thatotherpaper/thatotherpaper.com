Literate and unwashed: Kramer Wetzel

20 July 2007 | Luna
courtesy Kramer Wetzel Kramer Wetzel 

For Kramer Wetzel, astrology is more than a hobby – it’s a way of life. The founder of Astrofish.net doesn’t fit the stereotypical image of an astrologer – in place of sequined robes and pointy hats, he wears Hawaiian shirts and a cowboy hat. (The hair is dead on, though – long, light, and wavy.) And instead of a creaky old house with a sign offering palm readings on the door, he has a website titled “The Fishing Guide to the Stars,” a profitable astrology business, two books, a blog, a podcast, and a tour. He also does occasional professional consultations – for example, KEYE recently talked to him for their news piece on the magical implications of 7/7/07.

That Other Paper’s Luna recently chatted with the peerless prognosticator.

That Other Paper What is the purpose of your website?

Kramer Wetzel Unlike some websites, I started mine with no real clear purpose or vision, just an idea. The original site was first hosted, back in 1994, as an experiment in programming by some guy at Microsoft, sort of an unpublished version or first draft. By 1995, I was running it on a server in Austin. I was already writing a monthly horoscope, used in a couple of small publications. I started doing the weekly because it was more fun. That weekly got picked up by AOL, and the rest is clouded in the shrouds of history.

In the past dozen years, I’ve written daily, weekly, monthly, and yearly horoscopes. I never did find someone who had a blend of Texas and Austin-style horoscopes, you know, something a little peculiar, with a definite Texas flavor.

A client visited me from California, and she was amazed at the number of Lone Stars, state flags, and Texas this and that in Austin. Apparently other states are not as self-absorbed as Texas. Not that it matters, as the bulk of my readers are not from Austin. Or even Texas. Looking for that special flavor, I stuck with just doing this. It’s more like a search for the definitive band, that one place with good barbecue, or the perfect chicken-fried steak. It’s more about a journey and less about a destination.

TOP Can you explain what “Fishing Guide to the Stars” means?

KW In the lake we call Life, we can all use a little guidance. A good fishing guide can help point you in the right direction, and a really nice guide will bait your hook and clean the fish, too.

TOP How is it different than other astrology websites?

KW Who can combine Shakespeare’s Richard III with an old Ford truck and have the metaphor make sense? Literate and unwashed. What one would expect from the south side of town.

TOP What does Astrofish mean?

KW It’s a combination of astrology and a fishing guide to the stars, with a nod towards other interests, the whole fishnet thing.

TOP How did you get into astrology? How did you decide to build a website?

KW I have a degree – two – in English. Medieval lit is shot through and through with astrology references. Then, too, from years in the bar business, I know what a full moon does to drinkers.

When I started seriously writing horoscopes, as much for my own amusement as for anything else, I found a niche that I liked. When I first moved to Austin, I was returning to Dallas about once a month to see clients, but after a year in Austin, I managed to eke out an existence on just astrology readings. The rest is history.

TOP Do you make a living off of your website?

KW Yes. I’ve had several odd jobs, but as of now, the site is the only point of contact that I have, and it’s the main focus. In order to make a full-time living, I have to tour incessantly during the peak seasons, then I have to maintain a website that is constantly changing and evolving, and there’s always the chariot of change, nipping at my heels.

TOP What can you tell me about your demographic?

KW The horoscopes and the blog seem to draw a different crowd, with the horoscopes themselves more popular by a factor of 10. When I worked with AOL, I was assured by an editor that the demographics for the horoscopes I wrote were 90% female, between 24 and 40, divorced, one kid. Which is odd, while I’ve been told that the horoscope readers are largely female, I’ve discovered that my site pulls a more even number: half male, half female. Age is all over the place, from 20 to old.

TOP Tell me the story about the sentence on your bio page about why you think you were Shakespeare in a previous life and how you came to that conclusion.

KW An editor stuck that in, due, no doubt, to my propensity to quote Shakespeare’s canon.

TOP You also say in your bio that Texans are descended from a race of space aliens. Explain.

KW The hats, the boots, the accents – if you haven’t lived amongst us long enough, you wouldn’t get it.
