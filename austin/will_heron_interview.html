The shirt off his back

30 January 2007 | Kristin Hillery
Related Links
  • Will Heron
That Other Paper chats with local artist and T-shirt designer Will Heron about cheeseburger pizzas and not voting for Taylor Hicks

That Other Paper So what’s your story?

Will Heron I’ve answered this question in email before, so I just searched my hard drive for the word “story” and came up with this picture that I did for a friend’s birthday card.

photo / Will Heron 

But I’m guessing that’s not what you meant by “What’s your story?” So, um, my story in respect to shirt creation:

Christmas 2001, I was out of work, so I had a lot of free time and not too much cash. I wanted to make gifts for my nieces and nephews (I had six then, now seven), and I wanted to do it for cheap – for ease of operation and to keep costs low. Having recently completed a freelance gig for Levi’s designing kids’ T-shirts, I decided that they’d all get the same thing: homemade T-shirts. But I didn’t know how to silkscreen. For Levi’s, I just drew the pictures, and someone else made the shirts.

To the rescue were my friends Sharon Dang and Peat Duggins – both were instrumental in passing along their silkscreening knowledge to me. I shot the screens in my bathroom closet and washed them out in my shower. Once you make the screen, it’s easy to print more, so my friends and family all got tees for Christmas that year, too (and have for every gift-giving occasion since – I’m sure they’re sick of it by now).

I heard plenty of “you should sell these” comments, but the great catalyst was when another friend, Leigh-ann Jackson, told Laura Maxwell at Creatures Boutique that I would bring some tees by the store. At that point, if I didn’t do it, I would make Ms. Jackson look bad. Thank goodness Laura liked my stuff, and Creatures has been carrying it ever since.

In 2004, I converted an old storage garage into my studio, so I can now work full time from my home studio. The shirts are available in a handful of boutiques across the country and, of course, directly from me on the web. I LOVE to draw. T-shirts are sort of an after thought. The shirt is really just the medium through which I can sell my doodles. If I could sell my drawings any other way, I’d be open to that.

Sorry, I’m not much of a story teller.

TOP What got you into art and design?

photo / Will Heron A paper cut-out illustration by Will Heron 

WH I’ve always enjoyed drawing, as long as I can remember. The earliest original work I remember was at age 9 or 10, when my family took a trip to see our cousins in Italy. My mom wanted me to keep a journal, but I didn’t want to write. So she encouraged me to draw a journal instead. I made a different comic strip for each highlight of the trip. My mom has always encouraged my creative side, but it wasn’t until after graduate school that it even occurred to me that i could make a living at it. I mean, artists are broke, right?

On my second interview out of school (which was after I got my undergrad in art history, worked as a chef, and went back to school for advertising), I took in my portfolio and the interviewer said, “This is not an art director’s book, this is an illustrator’s book.” I told him that’s really what I wanted to do, so he suggested that I go do it. I did: Two days later, I had a job as an illustrator at a local interactive textbook company. That was 1998.

TOP On your website you have a section dedicated to your illustrations. Are some of them done with construction paper? What’s your favorite way to illustrate?

WH Yes, some of the illustrations up there are paper cut-outs. I used to love to do that. From ’96-’01, I did a lot of that cut paper work. I really like the look of it. Some of that cut paper stuff was instrumental in getting me that Levi’s gig, but I haven’t touched the stuff since then. Not because I don’t like it – I guess I just sort of forgot about it.

I’m not sure I could pick a favorite illustration method. I am quite fond of colored pencils and black fine-point pens. That’s usually where my ideas start, then I scan them into the computer and clean them up in Adobe Illustrator. Some drawings start out in Illustrator, but not many.

TOP Do you wear the T-shirts you design?

WH Of course! Today I wore my cheese T-shirt under a hoodie version of the “brr” penguin. My dresser is stuffed with no less than 25 of my own tees. Vain? Perhaps. Free advertising? Definitely.

TOP I noticed you sell a T-shirt that says “I didn’t vote for him either.” By “him,” do you mean current American Idol Taylor Hicks?

WH That’s a good one.

On the American Idol tip, I had a customer who bought the Texas skull shirt and told me he was Kelly Clarkson’s bassist and the shirt was a gift for her. I didn’t know who Kelly Clarkson was. I don’t watch a lot of TV other than some occasional sports.

photo / Will Heron “’05 Burger Family Reunion” shirt by Will Heron 

On the for-serious-voting tip, I made that shirt only a couple of days after “election” day, and it really wasn’t clear who had won. It was meant to be 100% ambiguous. Now years later, in the current political climate, everyone assumes you are taking sides. I think guns and war are stupid, but I’m not really a political person by nature.

TOP Describe the process of getting your T-shirts made, from the initial idea to the actual production of the shirt.

WH Idea –> sketchbook –> scan to computer –> refine and reduce in Illustrator –> make a transparency of the image, and then this.

TOPWhat’s next for you?

WH Seriously? I have no idea. I’m not the kind of guy who has my whole life mapped out. I’ve been very fortunate in life up to this point: Each small success has been followed by another. My business is slowly growing, so maybe someday it’ll be a giant T-shirt empire. I’d love to have the world domination that Paul Frank Industries has. Have you seen their latest collaboration with Lego? Some of it’s kinda funny.

But Paul Frank the Man doesn’t even work for Paul Frank the Industry anymore…. So who knows what that means. I’m just a doodler, not a dominator. I need a partner who’s a marketing whiz – or at least a kick-ass clothing rep who wants a super-cute line. Anyone? Anyone?

But what’s next for me? Hmmm…. Pizza? A cheeseburger? Maybe a cheeseburger pizza?
