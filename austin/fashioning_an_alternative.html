Fashioning an alternative

23 January 2007 | Kristin Hillery
photo / Kristin Hillery Mary Margaret with Rick, one of her students 

Mary Margaret Quadlander wasted no time making alterations to a small retail space on South Congress once she signed the lease for it last June. The previous renters ran a bike shop, and Mary Margaret inherited its blue faux-granite linoleum floors scuffed with skid marks and scratches. So she painted over it. Twirling yellows and spiraling reds, white splotches and sparks of orange are the result – the floor has been transformed into something fabulous that’s, well, only fitting for the Austin School of Fashion Design. The rest of the small space is filled with racks of vintage clothes, yards of fabric, rows of sewing machines, sketches, photos, and handmade cards.

The cards are from Mary Margaret’s former students at the University of Texas at Austin, where she taught Textiles and Apparel courses for three years. Three long, unhappy years of feeling creatively starved left her 30 pounds heavier.

“What UT’s teaching is Home Economics 101,” says Mary Margaret, shaking her head. “I think they want to eventually just teach textile science. But where will the creativity be? They should just axe the program.”

And if they do follow her advice, she’d welcome students to her school with open arms. In fact, a few UT Textiles and Apparel students are enrolled in her morning classes – and they head back to campus afterward just in time for Home Economics 101.

“They did me a favor when they said ‘This isn’t going to work,’” Mary Margaret remarks as she removes pins holding a skirt in place on a mannequin. She’s referring to her former supervisors at UT’s College of Natural Sciences. “So I came over here last June and opened the school.”

Mary Margaret’s certainly qualified to run this place – she’s currently working on a Master’s in liberal arts from St. Edwards, and she holds degrees in psychology (from UT) and fashion design (from New York’s FIT). She almost pursued a career in architecture but came back to her passion of fashion after it occurred to her that “building is building” no matter the medium. Good call – throughout her 20 years as a fashion designer and head of her own company, her designs were sold at stores like Neiman-Marcus and Cache.

photo / Kristin Hillery Austin School of Fashion Design 

“She’s great. She’s just great. She’s an inspiration,” raves Rick, one of Mary Margaret’s students. He’s taken a few courses at the Austin School of Fashion Design, and tonight he’s here for the first day of a class about draping.

There are only two students enrolled, so they’ll each get plenty of attention from Mary Margaret. “This is the perfect class size – I love this,” she says at the start of class.

It’s worth noting that the enrollment for this course doesn’t speak at all for the school’s success – 10 students were in the very first class when the school opened, and a total of 43 students are enrolled in courses this semester. Mary Margaret has never advertised, but she realizes her school offers people access to an art that’s recently seen a huge increase in popularity.

“Women’s liberation made us say we weren’t going to sew or make coffee or anything, but all those cooking shows and Project Runway have made it fun again – lots of people want to see if they’re the next big designer,” explains Mary Margaret.

But for many students at the Austin School of Fashion Design, this is more than just a fad – they come here to learn everything from basic sewing to how to create and market a collection for retail. Mary Margaret emphasizes, “This is a four-year degree.”

Mary Margaret’s students are definitely committed – almost as much as she’s committed to them.

“In the 6pm–8pm classes, we’ve never been kicked out of here at 8,” Rick says as he looks at the clock. It’s almost 8:30. “She always stays with us.”
