The Trip Fives: Kansas City improv hits Austin

31 August 2007 | Luna

Kansas City improvisers The Trip Fives traveled all the way from Missouri to participate in the Out of Bounds Improv Festival. They’re performing tonight, August 31, at 10pm at the Hideout Theater. That Other Paper Associate Editor Christine Acker chatted with member Jared Brustad.

That Other Paper How’d your troupe form?

Jared Brustad Back in 1996, we collectively performed with the now-defunct Lighten Up Improv Company. Tim and Ed have known each other since high school, as have Bryan and I. Trish [Berrong] was our very first director at Lighten Up. We were with Lighten Up until 1998. After that, some former cast members formed a new troupe known as Funny Outfit. That group existed for barely a year and a festival. At that point people drifted. Some of us got married, some of us had children, some of us got jobs, and one of us joined the Air Force. We all hadn’t performed together in nearly six years. Then Ed, the one that joined the Air Force, served his time and came back town craving to get back on stage. He made a few phone calls, people said yes, and we had our first show in June of 2006.

Even though The Trip Fives is a pretty newly formed group, the cast has up to 10 years of experience performing together. We feel that’s what gives us a slight edge over other local troupes here.

photo courtesy The Trip Fives 

TOP What makes your troupe stand out?

JB We stand out in Kansas City because we consider ourselves the anti-Whose Line Is It Anyway? group. No games. No gimmicks. Just straight-up improvisation.

TOP How’d you get involved in Out of Bounds?

JB We’ve heard many great things about past Out of Bounds festivals on the Yesand.com boards and great things about the improv scene in Austin as well. We put together a submission tape after our last local festival and sent it off.

TOP What’s the comedy scene like in your hometown?

JB The improv scene is slowly growing. Right now there are around 10 troupes that perform on a regular basis and a few others that perform sporadically. A couple of people created a not-for-profit organization called the City3 Project dedicated to creating a improve community. They’re currently producing monthly improv shows and a local theater and they even have a website where the public can find out about future shows of any troupe in town.

TOP What the story behind your troupe’s name?

JB Some of us in the group are big poker fans. We used to play in a local tournament every week. Trips is a poker term meaning three-of-a-kind, which is also called a set. There were five of us performing. An improv format could also be called an improv set. So, we are five people performing an improv set. A set of fives. Trip Fives. It sounds stupid when I explain it. Now I hate it. Thanks.

TOP What’s your worst or most awkward improv moment?

JB Describing the story behind our name. Just kidding. I don’t know. Experiencing horrible moments in improv can only make you a better performer. I think when all of used to perform in our early 20s we played very surreal. We thought of ourselves as shocking. We’ve grown and matured since then for the better.

photo courtesy The Trip Fives 

TOP What school of improv do you practice?

JB Over time we’ve all taken classes from some of the best: Mick Napier, Del Close, Armando Diaz, Dan O’Conner, Jeff Wirth, Joe Bill and Mark Sutton, only to name a few. Trish Berrong (who taught us all), on the other hand, goes up to Chicago frequently for the Chicago Improv Festival and to take classes and such. Then she brings her wisdom back to KC for all of us to bask in.

TOP What have you learned from improv and how has it applied to your life?

JB Life is improv and vice versa. Some of my best scenes have been with the stranger behind me in the check-out line.

TOP What do you like best about improv?

JB That feeling you get right before you hit the stage, knowing that you’re about to perform for an audience, and you have no clue what you’re going to say.

The Trip Fives are Jared Brustad, Megan Mercer, Trish Berrong, Tim Lemke, and Ed Doris.
