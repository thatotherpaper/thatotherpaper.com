Two minutes with a female volunteer firefighter

19 March 2008 | Christine Acker
courtesy MacKenzie Kelly / all rights reserved MacKenzie’s first fire 

MacKenzie Kelly is a vet clinic at Banfield Pet Hospital by day, but at night she transforms into one of the only female volunteer firefighters around town. (She asked that we not disclose exactly which Austin suburb’s fire department she volunteers for.) That Other Paper’s Christine Acker sat down with MacKenzie to learn about volunteer firefighting and what it’s like being one of the few women in the field.

That Other Paper How did you get into volunteer firefighting, especially as a female?

MacKenzie Kelly I used it as another way to get away from my parents when I was in high school. I was taking health and science classes and I saw a post about firefighting in the Explorer program, which is a program designed to expose high school students to different career paths. I wanted to explore it because I thought it would be really cool.

TOP What was entailed in this firefighting class?

MK Basically, once or twice a week we would meet before the actual volunteers with the fire department would meet. We would do the same training that the volunteer firefighters would do, which was fun. We got to ride out of the fire station in the fire trucks. We wouldn’t actually get to run calls, but we would be able to help at calls. That made me want to become an actual volunteer firefighter.

TOP Do you get to operate the sirens?

MK I get to operate the sirens sometimes.

TOP What’s involved in training to become a volunteer firefighter?

MK You have to do so many hours of basic firefighter training. It’s mostly hands-on stuff. We did stuff like ladder raising – the proper way to raise a ladder and how to tie it so it doesn’t fall down on someone. We did self-contained breathing apparatus training. We had to learn to put those on in under a minute. After I finished training, they gave me a voice pager, and I could respond to calls.

TOP Were there any minimum strength requirements or anything?

MK I don’t think so. We had to do everything the guys had to do.

TOP Did you have to haul 200-pound men or anything like that?

MK I did have to haul around a dummy once. The guys were like, “Let’s go, Firefighter Kelly! Come on!” And so I pulled him out of the fire with some rope. I used the California Love Knot.

TOP So have you ever gone to a real fire?

MK Oh yeah. I’ve been at five or six fires. My first fire was right after I became a volunteer. We were at a Monday night meeting, so all of the volunteers were there, about 20 of us plus the regular staff. We heard the sirens that meant we got a call, and then we heard, “Confirmed structure fire,” and the address of a house. Everyone got up at the same time and ran to the trucks. I got to ride officer seat in a rescue truck, and I was in charge of looking out the right window to make sure cars weren’t coming. The truck was doing 70 miles per hour down this road that my mom used to go 30 down. The way that the lights hit the buildings and the way people would look at you and just stop their cars – it was amazing.

We could see the fire from a block away. We had a mutual aid agreement with other fire departments that because we’re all kind of small we help each other out. All of our trucks were there and several from the other fire departments. It was crazy. The wife found out about the fire when she was upstairs in her closet, which was right next to the chimney, and she was like, “It’s really hot in here,” and at that same moment, her neighbors were downstairs banging on the door going, “Your house is on fire!”

TOP Wow.

MK Yeah. I’m trying out to work for the Austin Fire Department full-time on August 30th. The Austin testing process is a lot different from other testing processes because there are so many people who want to try out. Last year when I tested there were about 2,000 people. They rent out the Austin Convention Center and set up tables and they have you take a written test. If you pass the written test, you take the physical test. If you pass the physical test, they make you do some sort of lie-detector test. It’s pretty intense.

TOP Have you faced any sort of gender discrimination?

MK Not at all. We all joke around, but it’s never anything bad. They treat me like I’m their little sister.

TOP What’s your favorite part of volunteer firefighting?

MK The best part by far is the public education I get to participate in. I tell kids about fire safety and they actually listen. That’s better to me than fighting fire. Once, when I was putting my gear on, this girl yelled, “Hey lady, can’t your hair catch on fire?”
