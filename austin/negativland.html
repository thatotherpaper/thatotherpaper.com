It’s all in your head: Negativland show review

24 April 2008 | Kelly Handran
photo / groovehouse Taken at a Negativland performance at Rice University 

Negativland
April 19 | Alamo Drafthouse at the Ritz

Get this: Before last weekend, I’d never been to 6th Street on a Saturday night. My reason for finally going? I went to see the rarely-ever-performing group Negativland at the Alamo Ritz.

I splurged and paid for parking under I-35 and started my hike to the theater. It was just before 9pm – the downtown nightlife was starting to buzz, springing to life with scantily clad girls, punk rockers, and frat boys. As I neared the Ritz, I walked faster to hastily assimilate into the “free zone” of Negativland enthusiasts (and because I was seeing a rapidly swelling line of those enthusiasts).

I thought I had arrived early for will call ticket pickup, but actually I was just in time to stake my place in line before it snaked around the corner. The energy was brain-tingling. It seemed like every super-outsider nerd in town was symbolically lining up and forming the archetypal rock concert pre-event stance. This can’t be, I thought, due to the atypical fan and because Negativland consists of no conventional musical instruments, only machinery devised to make audio sounds (mostly of the pre-recorded record and tape variety). The anxiety of this diverse grouping of outcast geekoids increased, and as we all awaited the opening of the doors for the performance, discussions of what we were about to experience were being hurled around:

Weirdo 1

I heard they’re only going to talk as the performance.

Weirdo 2

The visuals will surely be surreal.

Weirdo 3

It’s like going to see the Wizard of Oz. I can’t wait to see what’s behind the curtain.
photo / ioerror A Negativland show audience member wears a mask during a performance at the Great American Music Hall 

Like a soda can opening fiss-spew, the crowd pressure released as the theater doors opened. We all marched into the building, grabbing our seats and readying ourselves for the event by placing our drink orders. Onstage was a long table draped in black with turntables, mixers, microphones, radio equipment, and prominently centerstage a light box sign “ON AIR.” As the lights dimmed, two spotlights hit the stage, and three men wearing collared shirts and ties migrated to their equipment setup. The rules of engagement were announced: The audience had to don the pinata-looking masks that were handed to us at the door. Tonight’s event would be auditory; the purpose of wearing the mask was to heighten our experience.

Our narrator, Dr. Oslo Norway, began the radio broadcast for the It’s In Your Head program. Members of the audience were deemed Headites and were challenged into processing the upcoming sounds. For the next two to three hours, unreal soundscapes were produced in a radio broadcast format, indulging tape loops, scratching records, and abnormal sound effects, all embellished with well written dialogue. The doctrine proposed: Toss out monotheism, yet still hold on to the Golden Rule. Loops of screeching “There is no God” layered with the most sickly sweet ’50s era Jesus songs (sung, of course, by children) mixed against intellectual sermon like scientific dogma combined, pushing my grey matter into a convulsive state.

photo / ioerror  

Christianity took the brunt of the astute de-structuring, though Islam, Judaism, and every other monotheistic culture based on “group thought” got their share of shattering. Atheism never looked so appealing.

As the show wrapped, I had a new found appreciation – not for religion, but for the precise technical mechanics it took in order for these three mortal men (truly the Wizards of Oz) to produce such an enlightening audio encounter. I tucked away my mask and proceeded directly to the merchandise table to purchase as much as I could possibly afford. With my arms full and my brain throbbing, I exited the theater into the hedonistic-Christian-redneck-party-nightlife of 6th Street, concretely cementing my epiphany of a Headite existence before me.
