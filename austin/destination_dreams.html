Destination: Dreams

29 November 2006 | Aaron Stanush and James Ashworth
photo / Chris Harrison 

Good morning, Integrated Accounting Systems employees! What was that? I can’t hear you! Let’s try this again. Good morning! No? Did we run out of coffee in the break room? Late night at the old watering hole? Third time’s a charm. Good morning!

Nothing? Well, we’ll see if that changes by the end of the seminar.

I’m here today to talk to you about power. What if I told you that you are capable of radiating energy so powerful that even your most haunting fears could be overcome in the time it takes to say “I can”?

Take my word for it: Each and every person in this auditorium has the power to alter the course of human history.

I know what you’re thinking. “Mr. Graverobber, I’m just a middle-manager at a software company that produces accounting systems for pipeline manufacturers. What could I possibly have within me that could affect the world?” Well, I have two responses to that. One: attitude is potential. Restlessness is inspiration. Desire is power. If you don’t think you deserve to live a better life, get out of your chair and get the hell out of this auditorium. Take your packet of course materials and your blueberry or chocolate chip muffin and get out of my damn presentation. Leave your raffle ticket, though – content people never win.

My second response to that question is this: I bet we’re all pretty glad that a certain person from history who changed the course of civilization didn’t feel that way. One man, whose odds were far worse and whose challenges were far more demanding than our own, didn’t give up in the face of adversity. This person persevered when everyone told him he was crazy. He didn’t give up in the face of failure, and his determination wasn’t diminished by the massive obstacles he encountered at every turn.

That’s right. I’m talking about Moses.

Everyone in this room has the ability to change his community for the better. You there, with the Seinfeld tie – what’s your name? Jack? What do you wish you had done differently in your life, Jack? You wish you had taken today off? Well, while Jack the comedian laughs himself into a coma over there, I’ll let you in on a little secret: I have some regrets. That’s right, that’s right – I’m no perfect specimen of the human species. Let me tell you a story.

After I won three state wrestling championships in college, to say I was a little cocky would be an understatement. I thought I was the cat’s pajamas. During the last semester of my senior year, I was given the opportunity to audition for a character role in a little organization known as the World Wrestling Foundation. They were a small crew of traveling athletes who were part wrestlers and part entertainers. The gig sounded decent, and the pay would have been more than enough to live on, but I passed it up. Why? I thought I was entitled to something better. No, I told them that the Chainsaw Charlie position sounded great but wasn’t exactly what I was looking for.

Fast forward 10 years.

photo / Brad Douglas 

I’m waiting tables in Chattanooga, Tennessee by day so I can work out at night and audition for pro-wrestling parts on the weekends. I’m midway through my shift on the Friday before Thanksgiving – I’m counting the minutes before I can go home. My sister has scored me some tickets to the WWF match that night, and I am hoping to pick up some new moves to impress the recruiting agents that I had heard would be at the YMCA that weekend. I’m bussing a table when in walks my arch nemesis – the man whom I had faced four times during college and never beaten – Jason Easton. His outfit? A hockey mask and a bloody shirt. That’s right: The part I had given up 10 years earlier, the character that had gone on to become a million-dollar franchise and the face of professional wrestling, had been offered to my worst enemy. I was crushed.

But from that devastation grew a fantastic opportunity. I attended the match that weekend and carefully surveyed Chainsaw Charlie’s moves for a weakness that could be exploited. I noticed that he left his abdomen exposed while executing the triple suplex. A quick jab or elbow to the gut would disable the character and allow for a reversal of his momentum. I had discovered my Theory of Relativity.

I presented my findings to the talent scouts at the YMCA the next day as a possibility for a change in guard. Chainsaw Charlie had ruled the ring for nearly a year, and the audiences were growing tired of his predictable victories. If I, a dark-horse wrestler with no history and no known weaknesses, could use this tactic to dethrone the reigning champion and claim his place as the king of wrestling, the entire industry would experience a revival. The scouts loved it, and I left the portion of the communal shower that we had designated as our conference room that day with a new identity: The Graverobber.

My match with Chainsaw Charlie three weeks later was completely unannounced. The WWF executives thought it would be best to surprise the audience at Revere, Massachusetts with a mysterious figure whose power was immeasurable and whose motives were unknown. Jason Easton had been given his script only three hours before the match began, but I like to think that his defeat was years in the making. Destiny had served itself, and the Graverobber became the premiere name in wrestling for the next six months.

The message I’d like to leave with you today, ladi– gentlemen, is that perception is nothing. Imagination is inspiration. Anxiety is satisfaction. Power is destiny. On the map of life, remember to orient your compass to the sky. On this journey we call fortune, the destination is our dreams.
