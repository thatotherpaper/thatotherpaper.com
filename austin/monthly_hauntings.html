Monthly hauntings

18 March 2008 | John Michael Cassetta
photo / Gin That’s how we became the Haunting Oboe Music bunch 

There’s an unnerving silence to the vaulted hallways of the UT music building that makes me feel unwelcome. The feeling is not completely unreasonable; I’m interviewing a band who is literally forcing itself to push the limits of its own creativity here, in the building that stands as a beacon for the time-honored standards of musical traditions. So we keep our voices hushed in hopes that our talk of experimental rock music won’t be overheard by the spirits of Haydn or Beethoven.

Haunting Oboe Music has put before itself a challenge of almost unthinkable proportions. It’s simple enough in its construction – 12 months, 12 EPs; one for each month, one for each letter in the first two words of its name. The idea came from the Brooklyn indie band Bishop Allen who did the same thing back in 2008. Haunting Oboe Music’s lead singer George (it’s first names only with these guys) had heard about Bishop Allen’s project and brought it to the band. “We looked at it as having two benefits,” says Jared, one of the band’s drummers the bassist, about the initial idea, “one, getting lots of press, getting our name out there as much as possible, and two, forcing ourselves to write a lot of stuff.”

The press has certainly been there. After only two EPs, the band has been featured in several local music publications. And of course, here I am generating press for them, myself. Needless to say, the band has been “getting its name out there” quite efficiently. But it should only be obvious that undertaking such a project would garner attention; as much as we love music, we love drama more, and nothing generates drama like a challenge. “Even though the concept itself is interesting, you want to make sure the material itself is interesting,” says Nick, the other drummer. This sentiment couldn’t be more true. We’re all interested in the concept – that’s what brought me to the music building to talk to the band – but as they say, don’t talk the talk if you can’t write and record a dozen solid EPs this year.

January’s EP is a testament to that creative power; it covers a broad range of musical themes from the soothing drone of feedback on “Kingdom” to the haunting (surprise, surprise) sounds of a screeching organ in “Mano The Soldier Boy.” Already the band has been compared to Radiohead, but when I asked them how they felt about the idea they scoffed, saying, “Post-Kid A, it’s hard to make an electronic album or anything with layered vocals or real airy stuff. That album changed so many people’s lives. It killed it.” Fair enough, so let’s look at the album objectively. With two drummers, the first obvious quality is the sheer complexity of the rhythmic background. On top of that you’ll find a driving bass, synthesizers, dystopian beeps and clicks, and layered vocals with the reverb piled on. Okay, that does sound a little bit like Radiohead (minus the $80 per show – really, Thom?), but in general there’s a more dissonant, almost caustic, presence than what the Radiohead comparison might suggest.

courtesy Haunting Oboe Music Haunting Oboe Music live 

Now with the idea of a “set” of EPs comes the idea of thematizing each one, but I’ve found that the band avoids the commercially viable option of dressing each month’s release in some cheesy and contrived theme (April: Showers or May: Flowers). Instead, each EP functions as a sample of that month’s work. As Ian, responsible for “guitars and horror,” puts it, “It’s like writing in a journal for one day. All those ideas will be inside that one day, so it’s going to have some sort of a feel because every day you have a different mood, and in that sense I think there’s a theme to it.” I could break out the flashy words and shiny metaphors for you here, but I think Ian’s simple comparison is far more accurate. Looking at bands across albums makes for a great analysis of how a band changes (I don’t know, say, between OK Computer and Kid A), but Haunting Oboe Music has put a system in place that samples its creative changes every month.

The band is aware of the litmus-test aspect of the project. Often when you spend four months on an album, says Jared, “you lose the spontaneity of the thing, it gets overworked and overproduced and it loses a lot of emotion.” By keeping all the production in-house, Ian notes, the band is “creating something and giving it directly to listeners.”

Even February’s EP gives a glimpse of this unfiltered sample of change. On “Violand,” the same haunting organ in “Mano The Soldier Boy” is present, but the sounds that build off of it are structured entirely different from the previous month’s songs. Violins and synthesizers intertwine and the repetitive vocals make for a very clean, though hardly polished, sound. There is also noticeably more trumpet throughout, compliments of keyboardist Jonathan, whom the other members assure me has been practicing like a madman to improve his horn skills.

So certainly there will be themes for each month, not unlike a diary, but the greater theme is the ability of listeners like you and me to have a musical archive of a band as they progress over an entire year. It’s the development of the band that is most captured by the EPs, but only when taken as a whole. And there are a whole lot left.

The pressure to be creative only increases as the year progresses. Two songs (“Behold, A Gremlin” and “Slavery in Space”) off of the January EP and one (“Girl Problems”) off the February EP were written prior to the project’s conception. “Once it gets to probably July or August, we’re gonna start havin’ to really bust ass in the month,” says Nick, well aware that the toughest months are yet to come, when all the songs must be written, developed, and recorded in the span of that month. But no hard feelings, boys, that’s your job – we’re just along for the ride.

Really, it’s a fantastic concept. When you have to wait two or three years for a new album to check in on how your favorite band is doing, you feel like Great Aunt Polly, squeezing your nephew’s cheeks: “Oh how you’ve grown! What have they been feeding you?” (A small price to pay for the big tin of popcorn she always gave me.) Why settle for that when Haunting Oboe Music gives us monthly visitation rights?

Now, when more and more bands are blogging about their daily activities (like defecation – ahem, Deerhunter), it’s nice to see a band that feels the need to share with us its changes through music. That kind of sharing is caring indeed.
